Executive framing: what changed

That is the immediate macro reset.

But the relief move sits on top of a still-dangerous physical-energy backdrop. So the tape improved faster than the underlying chokepoint risk did.

That gap between market pricing and physical-risk reality is the core regime fact this morning. You are not looking at a clean all-clear. You are looking at a market saying: the worst branch may not be the only branch anymore. That is enough for semis and long-duration growth to bounce, but not enough to justify lazy complacency.

The next policy anchor is not hypothetical. The Federal Reserve’s 2026 calendar shows the next FOMC meeting is scheduled for March 17–18. That means the market does not have a long runway to enjoy a macro fantasy. It has to carry this geopolitical-energy story straight into a major policy checkpoint.

The policy implication is simple: lower oil helps the Fed by reducing the risk that an energy shock feeds directly into near-term inflation expectations. But if the Hormuz disruption re-intensifies, the Fed problem comes back immediately. This is why today’s regime should not be read as “rates are solved.” It is closer to “the worst inflation tail temporarily eased.” Those are different claims. The first would justify a durable re-rating of long-duration assets. The second justifies a bounce, nothing more.

For investors, that means the policy path is still asymmetric. If oil stays contained into the FOMC window, the market can keep leaning into the idea that policy does not need to turn more restrictive. If oil re-spikes because the physical shipping issue worsens, the market quickly re-prices a nastier inflation/rates mix. The Fed itself does not need to do anything dramatic for that repricing to happen; the market will front-run it.

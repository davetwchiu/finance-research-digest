What changed vs. the old scaffold

This is no longer a filing inventory. It is a plain-English read-through of the most relevant names from the watchlist, centered on what actually matters to an investor now: what happened, the simple version, why the market should care, positives, red flags, and what to watch next.

Important limitation: SEC rendering/access was inconsistent in this environment. For RDW I was able to extract large parts of the primary 10-K directly. For several others I could confirm the filing itself and read portions of the primary filing, but not every exhibit/table cleanly. Where that happened, I say so explicitly instead of pretending to have full certainty.

Top takeaways

  • Redwire (RDW) delivered the clearest “real filing” signal: revenue rose with the Edge Autonomy acquisition, but margin quality worsened badly, losses deepened, impairments hit, and internal-control/material-weakness issues remain real.
  • Rocket Lab (RKLB) still reads like a scale story, not a finished-profit story. The annual report reinforces launch + space systems breadth, but execution and capital discipline remain the real test.
  • BigBear.ai (BBAI) and Palantir (PLTR) remain classic “AI/government narrative vs. fundamentals” filings: the important question is durability, concentration, and convertibility of demand into clean cash generation.
  • The 8-K set — ONDS, NVDA, AVGO, COHR — matters more for current market tape than deep long-term thesis. In practice these are mostly event/earnings-style updates, so the key question is whether they confirm demand strength or expose cracks in guidance, margin, or backlog.

Redwire (RDW) — 10-K

Read status: substantial primary filing read. This was the most fully readable filing in the set.

What happened

Redwire’s 2025 10-K shows a business that got bigger after acquiring Edge Autonomy, but not cleaner. Revenue increased 10% year over year, driven mainly by the acquisition, yet gross profit fell 61% and gross margin collapsed to 5% from 15%. The filing also shows impairment charges, heavy acquisition/integration costs, contract estimate problems, debt burden, and ongoing material weaknesses in internal control.

Layman version

Redwire bought growth, but the quality of that growth is messy. The company now has more defense/UAS exposure and a broader product set, but 2025 reads like a classic integration year where scale improved while financial quality got worse.

Why it matters now

RDW is trying to position itself as a serious space-and-defense platform just as U.S./European defense budgets and drone/autonomy spending are rising. That is the good story. The bad story is that the filing says execution is still rough, and the company is not yet earning the right to be treated like a clean compounder.

  • Positives: backlog of $411.2M; stronger defense exposure after Edge; two clearer segments (Space and Defense Tech); visible tailwinds from NATO/EU defense spending, missile defense, and drone demand.
  • Risks/red flags: 2025 had $41.1M of net unfavorable contract estimate adjustments, including a major loss reserve; gross margin only 5%; $34.7M impairment expense; material weaknesses remain; KPMG gave an adverse opinion on internal control; liquidity is usable but not luxurious relative to ambition and debt.
  • Watch next: whether management can turn Edge from a revenue booster into a margin improver, whether control weaknesses are actually remediated in 2026, and whether backlog converts without more ugly EAC revisions.

Rocket Lab (RKLB) — 10-K

Read status: partial primary filing read. I was able to access the annual report route and review portions, but not enough to claim a full clean extraction of all critical sections.

What happened

Rocket Lab filed its annual report, reinforcing that it is no longer just a small-launch company. The real business is increasingly a combination of launch, space systems, and platform infrastructure.

Layman version

This is a company trying to become a full-stack space prime rather than a one-product operator. That can create a much bigger business, but it also raises the execution bar.

Why it matters now

RKLB remains one of the market’s cleaner public ways to play broader space demand. The annual report matters because it forces investors to separate the attractive strategic story from the harder questions: when do scale, cadence, and product breadth turn into more durable economics?

  • Positive: diversified positioning is strategically attractive. The company is not relying on one narrow niche.
  • Risk/red flag: the more Rocket Lab tries to do, the more execution, schedule, and capital-intensity risks matter. The filing should be read with that lens, not just launch headlines.
  • Watch next: launch cadence, Neutron execution, space-systems margin quality, and capital needs versus internally generated cash.

Palantir (PLTR) — 10-K

Read status: partial primary filing read. I could confirm and access the annual filing, but did not get a sufficiently clean full-text extraction from the SEC page in this session.

What happened

Palantir filed its annual report, which is mainly a reality check on whether the company’s AI platform momentum is turning into a broader, more durable business rather than just a hot narrative.

Layman version

PLTR is no longer just a controversial government contractor and no longer just an “AI buzzword” stock. The filing matters because it shows whether the commercial side is broadening enough to support the valuation and whether customer adoption is becoming repeatable.

Why it matters now

Palantir’s valuation leaves little room for execution slips. Investors need the 10-K to keep focus on concentration risk, stock-based compensation, contract durability, and whether government strength is being matched by real commercial expansion.

  • Positive: PLTR remains one of the few software names with strong government credibility plus AI-enterprise relevance.
  • Risk/red flag: valuation risk is the big one. Even a healthy business can be a weak stock if expectations outrun what the filing supports.
  • Watch next: large-deal consistency, commercial customer expansion, margin discipline, and any signs that growth is relying too heavily on a concentrated set of customers or sectors.

Bottom line

The cleanest filing signal today came from RDW: bigger company, uglier financial quality, still very investable to watch but not yet fully de-risked. RKLB remains strategically attractive but execution-sensitive. PLTR and BBAI remain two very different versions of the same market test: can AI/government demand turn into durable economics rather than just narrative heat? The 8-K names matter most for immediate sentiment and guideposts, but for several of them the exhibit text needs a second verification pass before making any high-conviction call.

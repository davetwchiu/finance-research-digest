ONDS — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-09 · Company: Ondas Inc.

Verified: Read the 8-K header plus Exhibit 99.1 press release filed with the SEC.

What happened: Ondas said it signed a merger agreement with Mistral, a U.S. defense prime contractor. The filing says Mistral brings prime-contractor access, U.S.-based manufacturing infrastructure, and existing federal/state contracting relationships.

Layman version: This is Ondas trying to move up the defense food chain. Instead of mainly being a supplier into other people’s programs, it wants a faster path to become the company directly holding and managing more government work.

Why it matters now: For a small defense/drone name, distribution and contract access matter as much as the hardware. If this closes cleanly, Ondas could get closer to actual procurement budgets instead of just the story.

What changed vs before: The key change is strategic position: Ondas says the deal would add direct prime access and contract infrastructure that it did not have on its own before. This is still an agreement stage filing, not a completed-close filing.

Positives:

  • Potential shortcut into Department of Defense and public-safety procurement channels.
  • Mistral is described as having more than $1 billion of IDIQ and related contract exposure, which could expand Ondas’ addressable path to orders.
  • Adds U.S.-based manufacturing / contract administration capabilities.

Risks / red flags:

  • It is still a signed agreement, not proof of integration success or new revenue.
  • Small-company M&A can distract management and create execution risk.
  • Contract access does not automatically mean contract wins.

What to watch next: Watch for closing terms, financing/integration details, backlog or booked-order updates, and any follow-up disclosure showing Mistral actually improves award flow.

AVGO — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-04 · Company: Broadcom Inc.

Verified: Read the 8-K and the attached Exhibit 99.1 earnings release.

What happened: Broadcom reported fiscal Q1 2026 revenue of $19.311 billion, up 29% year over year, with adjusted EBITDA of $13.128 billion and free cash flow of $8.010 billion. It also guided Q2 revenue to about $22.0 billion, said Q1 AI revenue reached $8.4 billion and is expected to rise to $10.7 billion in Q2, and announced a new $10 billion share repurchase program.

Layman version: This was a strong earnings-style filing. Broadcom is telling investors the AI demand wave is still real, profits are staying huge, cash generation is strong, and management is confident enough to guide sharply higher and authorize more buybacks.

Why it matters now: This matters because AVGO is one of the cleaner AI-infrastructure readouts. The filing gives hard numbers, not hype, and the Q2 guide implies acceleration rather than a near-term air pocket.

What changed vs before: Versus the prior quarter narrative, the new thing here is acceleration: AI semiconductor revenue grew 106% year over year to $8.4 billion and management says it should reach $10.7 billion next quarter. The company also layered on a new $10 billion buyback signal.

Positives:

  • Revenue, EBITDA, EPS, and free cash flow all came in at very strong levels.
  • AI revenue growth accelerated and exceeded management’s own prior forecast.
  • Q2 guidance of roughly $22.0 billion suggests momentum is still building.

Risks / red flags:

  • Expectations are now higher; any future slowdown in AI networking/custom accelerator demand would matter more.
  • A very large share of the bull case is now tied to continued hyperscaler AI spending.
  • Buybacks are helpful, but they do not remove cyclicality risk if enterprise or AI budgets soften.

What to watch next: Watch whether Q2 delivery matches the $10.7 billion AI revenue outlook, whether infrastructure software stays stable, and whether margins remain near this unusually strong level.

KTOS — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-06 · Company: Kratos Defense & Security Solutions, Inc.

Verified: Read the primary 8-K filing body.

What happened: Kratos disclosed that it completed the Orbit acquisition on March 2. Orbit is now an indirect wholly owned subsidiary. The filing says Kratos paid about $352.7 million in cash, funded from balance-sheet cash, at $13.725 per Orbit share.

Layman version: This is the “deal is done” filing. Investors no longer need to guess whether the acquisition will close. Kratos has now actually spent the money and owns the asset.

Why it matters now: Completed deals matter more than announced deals. Once the acquisition closes, investors can start judging whether the purchase price, balance-sheet hit, and strategic fit make sense in real operating results.

What changed vs before: The November 2025 announcement was a plan; this filing says the transaction is now complete. Orbit has moved from external target to owned business, and the cash is gone from Kratos’ balance sheet.

Positives:

  • Removes closing uncertainty around the Orbit transaction.
  • Using existing cash avoids immediate new financing overhang in this filing.
  • Could expand Kratos’ ISR / defense systems capability set if integration goes well.

Risks / red flags:

  • The $352.7 million cash outlay raises the bar for synergy and returns.
  • Integration risk is now real rather than theoretical.
  • Future filings will need to show whether Orbit improves growth and margins enough to justify the price.

What to watch next: Watch the next quarter for pro forma commentary, integration costs, backlog changes, and management evidence that Orbit is helping bookings or margins rather than just increasing complexity.

NVDA — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-06 · Company: NVIDIA Corp.

Verified: Read the primary 8-K filing body.

What happened: NVIDIA’s compensation committee adopted its fiscal 2027 executive variable compensation plan. The filing says executive cash payouts will depend on fiscal 2027 revenue performance, with threshold/base/stretch levels. CEO Jensen Huang’s target opportunity at base plan achievement is listed at $4.0 million, or 200% of base salary.

Layman version: This is not an operating shock or product filing. It is a governance/pay filing showing that NVIDIA is still tying top executive cash incentives to revenue growth for the coming fiscal year.

Why it matters now: It matters mainly as a read on board confidence and incentives. If the board is using revenue-based targets again, it suggests management is still being measured against keeping the growth machine running.

What changed vs before: The new thing is the board has now formally set the fiscal 2027 compensation plan and performance structure. This is a fresh incentive framework for the new fiscal year, not a change to the underlying business this week.

Positives:

  • Incentives remain tied to a clear operating metric rather than purely time-based pay.
  • Signals continued internal focus on maintaining very high revenue performance.
  • No surprise financing or operational warning was disclosed in this filing.

Risks / red flags:

  • This filing does not add new demand data, product data, or margin data.
  • Revenue-linked incentives can encourage aggressive target setting and sustain very high market expectations.
  • It is only a secondary signal for investors compared with the actual earnings filings.

What to watch next: Watch future earnings and guidance filings to see whether the business trajectory supports the revenue targets implied by this compensation structure.

GOOG — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-06 · Company: Alphabet Inc.

Verified: Read the primary 8-K filing body.

What happened: Alphabet approved a new triennial CEO pay package for Sundar Pichai. The filing says his $2 million salary is unchanged and he still gets no annual bonus, but he received new equity awards including two PSU tranches with target values of $63 million each, $84 million of GSUs vesting over three years, plus Waymo and Wing performance units with target values of roughly $130 million and $45 million.

Layman version: Alphabet is reloading Pichai’s long-cycle stock package rather than changing his cash pay. The board is basically saying: keep the fixed salary flat, but let most of the upside depend on stock performance and progress in major bets like Waymo and Wing.

Why it matters now: This is mostly a governance/capital-allocation signal, but it also tells you what the board thinks matters strategically: core share performance plus monetization/execution in major moonshot businesses.

What changed vs before: The salary did not change. What changed is a fresh three-year equity package, replacing the prior cycle that was granted in 2022.

Positives:

  • Keeps cash salary flat while leaning on long-dated, performance-linked equity.
  • Ties part of the upside to relative shareholder return and to Waymo/Wing performance.
  • Shows the board still wants Pichai aligned with long-horizon value creation.

Risks / red flags:

  • The headline package size may still draw scrutiny even if much of it is performance-based.
  • This filing says more about governance than near-term operating momentum.
  • Waymo/Wing-linked incentives highlight that those projects still need to prove durable economic value.

What to watch next: Watch whether upcoming operating results and “Other Bets” disclosures show progress that supports the strategic priorities embedded in this pay package.

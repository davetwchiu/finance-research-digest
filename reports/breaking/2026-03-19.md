## Headline
IEA says 8 mb/d out of the Strait of Hormuz is offline and the 400 mb emergency release is only a stopgap
## Body
IEA’s March 2026 Oil Market Report lays out the latest count: the Middle East war has virtually closed the Strait of Hormuz, forcing Iraq, Qatar, Kuwait, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia to curb roughly 8 million barrels per day of crude plus another 2 million barrels of condensates/NGLs, with refineries and product exports also throttling as ship loading, insurance, and storage bottlenecks lock in the shortage. The agency notes member countries agreed to make 400 million barrels of emergency stock release available, but that is only a temporary bridge while shipping security and on-the-ground infrastructure remain impaired.
## Why it matters
That is the largest verified supply hole since the prior sanctions-driven shock, and it keeps the oil → inflation expectations → higher real and nominal yields transmission wide open. The scale of the disruption leaves little room for central banks to lean against the move without triggering volatility in other parts of the market, so the regime that crushed long-duration growth earlier this year is back in play.
## Watchlist impact
Direct — long-duration, AI/semi/capex-heavy names (NVDA, AVGO, TSM, MSFT, GOOG, AAPL, LITE, COHR, TSLA) now carry elevated risk-premium pressure because sticky oil flows keep yields and discount-rate worries on investors’ minds even if execution stays strong. Second-order — defense/space/autonomy plays (KTOS, PLTR, RDW, RKLB, ONDS, UUUU) may retain relative bids but still operate inside a broader risk-off tape, so the only clean alpha path is disciplined sizing around confirmed contracts rather than broad macro beta exposure.

Executive summary

  • Most useful filing on the page: AVGO. Broadcom’s 10-Q still reads like a scale-and-cash machine: $19.3B revenue, $13.2B gross profit, $7.35B net income, roughly $45B remaining performance obligations, and $7.85B of buybacks in one quarter. The main debate is no longer “is the engine real?” but whether valuation and leverage already price in too much durability.

  • Lower-signal filings should stay lower in your decision stack. NVDA and GOOG filed governance/pay disclosures, and KTOS filed acquisition-close confirmation. Those are worth knowing, but they do not carry the same operating weight as a fresh 10-Q/10-K read.

AVGO — 10-Q

Filed: 2026-03-11 · Company: Broadcom Inc. · Primary read

What the filing actually said: Broadcom reported fiscal Q1 revenue of $19.311B versus $14.916B a year earlier, $13.157B gross margin, $8.563B operating income, and $7.349B net income. Quarter-end cash and cash equivalents were $14.174B. The filing also disclosed about $45B of remaining performance obligations and $7.85B of repurchases during the quarter, with a fresh $10B buyback authorization in March.

Plain-English read-through: The business still looks exceptionally strong on hard numbers. This is not a quarter where you have to squint past accounting noise to see the thesis. Revenue scaled hard, margins stayed elite, and the company still has a very large pool of contracted work behind it.

Why investors should care: For a name priced on AI infrastructure durability, the key question is whether the cash machine is still showing up in filings, not just in management commentary. On that test, this 10-Q is solid. The remaining-performance-obligations figure matters because it gives real support to the idea that demand is not purely quarter-to-quarter.

What is genuinely bullish here: Strong growth at large scale, very high profitability, big cash generation, and a willingness to keep buying back stock aggressively. If you already own the name, this filing does more to support “stay with the winner” than to undermine it.

What keeps it from being a clean layup: Cash fell from $16.178B to $14.174B because Broadcom is spending hard on repurchases, and long-term debt is still large at $63.805B. This is a powerful business, but not a simple balance-sheet story. If AI enthusiasm cools or software durability disappoints, the market will start caring more about that leverage.

Bottom line: This filing strengthens the business-quality case more than it changes the debate. The real question after reading it is not “is Broadcom executing?” but “how much of this quality is already fully capitalized into the stock?”

ONDS — 8-K

Filed: 2026-03-11 · Company: Ondas Holdings Inc. · Primary filing plus attached official materials read

What the filing actually said: The 8-K registers 6,933,110 Ondas shares issued in the BIRD Aerosystems acquisition. On its own, that is a capital-markets / transaction-cleanup filing. But the attached official materials are the real reason this deserves attention: management frames BIRD as an airborne missile-protection and ISR platform deployed on 700+ aircraft across 40+ aircraft types.

Plain-English read-through: This was not a fresh operating update, but it did make Ondas’ strategic position easier to take seriously. Before, BIRD was mostly an acquisition headline. After reading the filing package, the combined story looks less like “small company buying a buzzword asset” and more like “small company adding a defense product base with some real deployment history.”

Why it matters: For a small defense-adjacent name, the difference between a speculative capability story and an already-deployed mission system matters a lot. If BIRD’s installed base and defense relationships are real and monetizable, Ondas has moved up a notch in credibility.

What not to overstate: This filing does not prove earnings accretion, margin quality, or smooth integration. It also introduces a real stock-overhang issue because the filing is explicitly about registering acquisition-related shares for resale. Strategic quality may have improved, but the equity story is still not clean.

Bottom line: Better company story, still early proof. The next important step is not another narrative deck; it is actual disclosure on BIRD revenue, backlog, margins, and whether Ondas can turn the installed-base story into repeatable defense orders.

Verification note: primary 8-K read directly and checked against the filing’s attached official materials. This section is intentionally strategic rather than financial because the filing package offered much more proof on installed capability than on current earnings contribution.

BBAI — 10-K

Filed: 2026-03-02 · Company: BigBear.ai Holdings, Inc. · Primary read

What the filing actually said: 2025 revenue was $127.7M, down from $158.2M in 2024. Gross profit fell to $28.5M from $45.2M. GAAP net loss was still huge at $293.9M. Free cash flow stayed negative at -$46.3M. At the same time, the balance sheet improved sharply: cash plus available-for-sale debt securities rose from $50.1M to $461.5M, and management said total debt fell from $142.3M at year-end to $17.7M after subsequent debt reduction. The company also closed the Ask Sage acquisition at year-end.

Plain-English read-through: This is a much healthier financing story than it was before, but it was not a better operating year. If you only looked at the “AI defense” narrative and the cleaner balance sheet, you could miss that the core income statement actually moved the wrong way in 2025.

Why it matters: Small AI-defense names often get re-rated on survivability before they get re-rated on execution. This filing says BBAI likely bought itself more runway and optionality. That matters. But the filing also says the business still has to prove that improved financial flexibility can become durable operating progress.

What looks constructive: The financing overhang is meaningfully less scary, Ask Sage gives the company a more current defense-AI asset, and backlog remained material at roughly $248M. The company disclosed only $8.2M of remaining performance obligations, which is modest, but that metric understates contract depth relative to backlog in this business model.

What still looks weak: Revenue fell 19.3%, cost of revenue remained heavy, and the loss structure is still ugly. A stronger balance sheet should not be mistaken for a proven operating turn. Until management shows that the newer AI/security positioning can lift revenue quality and margins, this remains more turnaround speculation than clean execution.

Bottom line: The 10-K improved the “will they survive and keep investing?” question far more than the “have they fixed the business?” question.

Verification note: primary 10-K text read directly. This writeup intentionally separates the balance-sheet improvement from the weaker 2025 operating performance instead of blending them into a generic “progress” story.

RKLB — 10-K

Filed: 2026-02-26 · Company: Rocket Lab Corp · Primary read

What the filing actually said: Revenue rose to $601.8M from $436.2M, up 38%. Gross profit improved to $207.2M from $116.1M, lifting gross margin to 34.4% from 26.6%. Space Systems revenue was $402.8M and Launch Services revenue was $199.0M; launch-services growth was helped by 21 completed missions versus 16 the year before. The company ended the year with $828.7M of cash and cash equivalents plus $1.10B of marketable securities.

Plain-English read-through: This is one of the better “growth still looks real” filings in the space bucket. Rocket Lab did not just add revenue; it also converted more of that scale into gross profit. That is important because launch and space systems can easily grow in ways that never become economically attractive.

What investors should focus on: The filing reinforces that Rocket Lab is no longer just an Electron-launch story. The Space Systems segment is now the larger revenue engine, while launch cadence still matters as a proof-of-execution signal. That combination is healthier than relying on launch alone.

Main caveat: The company is still losing money and remains in investment mode. The 10-K is also very explicit that Neutron execution risk matters. If Neutron slips badly, a meaningful piece of the future narrative gets pushed out while spending continues.

Bottom line: Of the space names on this page, RKLB looks like one of the cleaner examples of a company turning ambition into measurable commercial progress. It is still not a finished profit story, but the operating direction is better than the average speculative space filing.

Verification note: primary 10-K text read directly. I did not try to force a backlog conclusion because the filing text available in this pass was clearer on revenue mix, gross-profit progression, liquidity, and Neutron risk than on a clean backlog summary.

RDW — 10-K

Filed: 2026-02-27 · Company: Redwire Corp · Primary read

What the filing actually said: Revenue increased 10% to $344.0M from $312.7M, helped mainly by the Edge Autonomy acquisition. The company ended 2025 with $94.5M in cash and cash equivalents and said available liquidity totaled $129.5M including borrowing capacity. Total contracted backlog was $411.2M, including $299.8M in Space and $111.4M in Defense Tech. But the filing also showed a very large net loss attributable to Redwire of $(226.6)M and highlighted sizeable unfavorable estimate-at-completion adjustments.

Plain-English read-through: The top line and backlog say there is a real business here. The loss profile says it is still messy to run. This is the kind of filing where a bullish reader can find enough to stay interested, but not enough to claim operating discipline has arrived.

What matters most: Edge Autonomy gave Redwire more scale and a stronger defense-tech angle. That strategic broadening is real. But the filing makes clear that contract execution and cost estimation remain pain points. In aerospace and defense, those problems can eat the value of backlog very quickly.

Bottom line: Better strategic shape, still weak operating cleanliness. For now RDW reads more like a platform-building story with execution risk than a fully de-risked compounder.

Verification note: primary 10-K text read directly. This section stays narrower than AVGO/PLTR because the filing is more useful for backlog/liquidity/execution framing than for a clean profitability thesis.

PLTR — 10-K

Filed: 2026-02-17 · Company: Palantir Technologies Inc. · Primary read

What the filing actually said: 2025 revenue reached $4.475B, up 56%. Government revenue was $2.402B, up 53%, and commercial revenue was $2.073B, up 60%. Gross profit was $3.7B, or an 82% gross margin. Income from operations was $1.414B, and net cash provided by operating activities was $2.1B. Contract liabilities rose to $812.3M, and remaining performance obligations were $4.1B, with about 38% expected to be recognized over the next 12 months.

Plain-English read-through: This 10-K reads like a company that has already crossed from “story stock” into “serious enterprise machine.” The most important thing in the filing is not one contract or one product mention. It is that both government and commercial segments are scaling hard at strong margins.

Why it matters: The old knock on Palantir was that government concentration and stock-compensation optics could obscure whether there was a broad, durable business underneath. This filing makes that critique harder to lean on. Commercial is big now, government is still growing fast, and cash generation is heavy.

Main caution: After a filing this strong, the business case is easier than the stock case. A lot of future perfection may already be in the share price. Also, some Palantir contracts still have government-style cancellation flexibility, so backlog/RPO should not be treated as a simple SaaS-style lockbox.

Bottom line: One of the strongest fundamental filings on the watchlist. The risk after reading it is more about valuation discipline than about whether the company is genuinely executing.

Verification note: primary 10-K text read directly, including the contract-liability and remaining-performance-obligation disclosures.

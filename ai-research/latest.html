Last updated: 2026-03-14 17:55 HKT • Claims are limited to sources checked in this run. Facts, interpretation, and scenario assumptions are separated on purpose.

Executive framing

Bottom line AI is still an infrastructure, cloud-distribution, and workflow-control market first. Demand is unquestionably real. Broad software-layer margin capture is still much less proven than the narrative suggests.

  • Best-supported monetizers: NVIDIA and Microsoft.
  • Best-supported adoption signal: OpenAI’s business and seat growth, plus Accenture’s implementation bookings.
  • Most underpriced bottleneck: power and energization, not just chips.
  • Most important structural risk: policy is splitting the market by geography, compliance stack, and approved deployment paths.

1) Executive thesis and key calls

Core call The profit pool remains upstream. Usage is spreading faster than durable app-layer economics.

Facts

  • NVIDIA reported Q4 FY2026 revenue of $68.1B, Data Center revenue of $62.3B, and non-GAAP gross margin of 75.2%. It guided Q1 FY2027 revenue to $78.0B ±2%.
  • Microsoft reported FY26 Q2 revenue of $81.3B, Microsoft Cloud revenue of $51.5B, Azure growth of 39%, and commercial remaining performance obligation of $625B, up 110%.
  • OpenAI says it now has more than 1 million business customers, more than 7 million ChatGPT for Work seats, and more than 800 million weekly users.
  • Accenture said Q1 FY2026 advanced AI bookings were $2.2B and advanced AI revenue was about $1.1B.

Interpretation

  1. The argument is no longer about whether AI demand exists. It does.
  2. The live investment question is who keeps the economics after capex, inference, support, and compliance costs.
  3. Right now the strongest evidence still favors scarce compute, cloud control points, and embedded enterprise workflow positions.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes AI spending stays firm through 2026, but software/app winners remain selective rather than universal.

3) Demand analysis

Facts

  • OpenAI says it has more than 800 million weekly users.
  • OpenAI says it has more than 1 million business customers.
  • OpenAI says ChatGPT for Work seats reached more than 7 million, up 40% in two months; Enterprise seats are up 9x YoY.
  • OpenAI’s enterprise report says enterprise message volume grew 8x and API reasoning-token consumption per organization increased 320x year over year.
  • That same report says enterprise users report saving roughly 40–60 minutes per day.
  • Accenture’s advanced AI figures imply large enterprise buyers are paying for implementation, not just experimentation.

Interpretation

Demand has moved beyond proof-of-concept. The more important change is deeper workflow use, not just more prompt volume. But one important caveat: most of this evidence is still vendor-supplied. That supports the direction of travel, not blind confidence in every downstream software multiple.

My call: enterprise AI is real enough that the debate should shift from “is adoption happening?” to “how much of the value stack stays with the infrastructure owner, distributor, or workflow gatekeeper?”

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes production adoption stays concentrated in coding, research, support, search, and structured process automation rather than becoming evenly monetized across the whole software landscape.

4) Supply analysis

Facts

  • NVIDIA’s Q1 FY2027 revenue outlook of $78.0B ±2% implies no near-term collapse in AI infrastructure demand.
  • NVIDIA explicitly said it is not assuming any Data Center compute revenue from China in that outlook.
  • IEA estimates data-center electricity consumption at around 415 TWh in 2024 and projects it to roughly 945 TWh by 2030 in the base case.
  • IEA notes data centers can be operational in two to three years, while the broader energy system often needs much longer lead times.

Interpretation

The constraint is no longer just chip availability. It is usable capacity: power, cooling, interconnect, siting, and the ability to reserve supply early. That strongly favors hyperscalers and upstream winners over smaller challengers.

Strong view: power is now a first-order AI variable, not a footnote. Investors who only track model launches are following the showroom, not the factory.

Scenario assumptions

I am deliberately not making detailed packaging/foundry share claims in this edition because this run did not collect enough fresh primary evidence to support a higher-conviction supply-chain call at that level of granularity.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

  1. Physical moat: compute, energy access, networking, and capital availability.
  2. Distribution moat: cloud contracts, procurement trust, and installed enterprise footprint.
  3. Workflow moat: owning the system where work is approved, logged, and audited.
  4. Model moat: important, but less durable by itself than current market excitement implies.

My view is blunt: the market still talks as if the model is the business. In practice, the business is usually the distribution rail and workflow control point wrapped around the model.

This is why NVIDIA and Microsoft look structurally better supported than a broad basket of app-layer names, and why enterprise-service firms like Accenture still matter as a read-through on production conversion.

7) Financial architecture

Facts

  • NVIDIA’s Q4 FY2026 non-GAAP gross margin was 75.2%.
  • Microsoft Cloud revenue was $51.5B in FY26 Q2, with commercial RPO at $625B.
  • Accenture said cumulative advanced AI bookings reached approximately $11.5B and revenue reached approximately $4.8B to date.

Interpretation

The financial architecture of AI still looks top-heavy. Vendors with scarce assets and strong balance sheets can invest first, reserve supply first, and collect first. Services firms can monetize the migration. Much of the app layer is still proving whether AI revenue stays attractive after model costs, discounts, support, and control layers are included honestly.

I remain skeptical of software narratives that highlight engagement or seat growth without discussing gross margin after inference cost, renewal quality, or pricing durability.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes inference efficiency improves, but not fast enough to erase the scale advantage of the largest compute buyers and distributors.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

Facts

  • BIS revised export controls on advanced computing ICs and added a new control on AI model weights for certain advanced closed-weight dual-use models.
  • The rule was effective 13 Jan 2025, with a main compliance date of 15 May 2025 and some delayed provisions to 15 Jan 2026.
  • NVIDIA’s latest outlook assumes no China Data Center compute revenue.

Interpretation

Export controls are not headline noise anymore; they are part of industry structure. The strategic result is simple: AI is not one global market. It is becoming multiple policy-bounded markets with different chip access, deployment rules, and monetization ceilings.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes the U.S. keeps a hard line on frontier compute diffusion, with periodic tightening but not total paralysis of allied-market deployment.

9) Policy / regulatory landscape and likely pathways

Facts

  • European Commission says prohibited AI practices became effective in February 2025.
  • General-purpose AI obligations became applicable in August 2025.
  • High-risk AI rules come into effect in August 2026 and August 2027.
  • The high-risk stack includes risk management, dataset quality, logging, documentation, deployer information, human oversight, robustness, cybersecurity, and accuracy.

Interpretation

Regulation quietly strengthens incumbent enterprise platforms. Compliance is operational and continuous, not cosmetic. Vendors that can bundle model capability with logging, auditability, access control, and deployment governance should win more procurement decisions.

Scenario assumptions

I assume uneven early enforcement, but strong enough policy gravity to influence product roadmaps and buying criteria well ahead of the final deadlines.

11) Scenario tree for next 3 / 6 / 12 months

3 months

  • Base (55%): infrastructure leaders and cloud distributors continue to show the cleanest monetization; enterprise usage deepens, but broad app-layer revenue proof stays selective.
  • Bull (25%): several workflow vendors start posting measurable AI revenue with decent unit economics, broadening software confidence.
  • Bear (20%): power, policy, or capex-discipline worries slow deployment enough to expose weak downstream narratives.

6 months

  • Base (50%): spending stays strong but value capture remains concentrated in NVIDIA, hyperscalers, and major workflow/distribution rails.
  • Bull (20%): more production deployments expand beyond coding/search/support, validating a broader software capture layer.
  • Bear (30%): capex digestion and policy friction compress sentiment before downstream monetization catches up.

12 months

  • Base (45%): AI is economically large but still more concentrated than the broad equity narrative assumes.
  • Bull (25%): leading workflow/software vendors prove AI can be margin-accretive, not just engagement-accretive.
  • Bear (30%): the market learns that abundant model access without abundant cheap power and workflow ownership is a weaker business than expected.

What would change my mind

  • I would get more constructive on the app layer if multiple software vendors show sustained AI revenue growth with credible margin durability and low churn.
  • I would get less constructive on the upstream winners if capacity expansion normalizes faster than demand and margins compress without offsetting software/distribution monetization.
  • I would upgrade policy risk further if export controls broaden materially beyond current assumptions or if EU enforcement proves harsher and faster than expected.

Top caveats

  • Several demand datapoints come from vendor-reported sources, especially OpenAI. Useful, but not fully independent.
  • This edition intentionally avoids over-claiming on detailed packaging/foundry bottlenecks because fresh primary sourcing was not deep enough in this run.
  • The notebook is strong on industry structure and monetization hierarchy; it is weaker on precise valuation implications because this brief is sector-first rather than stock-model-first.

1) Executive thesis and key calls

Core thesis The AI industry is still in a real buildout, but the center of gravity has shifted. The question is no longer whether demand exists. It is whether the system can convert extraordinary demand into durable cash flow before capex discipline, power constraints, and policy friction bite.

  1. Near-term winners are still infrastructure-heavy, especially NVIDIA, Broadcom, TSMC packaging, and hyperscaler capacity owners. But the easy multiple expansion phase is behind them unless utilization and gross-profit conversion stay unusually strong.
  2. Enterprise adoption is broadening, but production value is uneven. OpenAI and Anthropic data both point to deeper usage, yet much of the volume still clusters around coding, knowledge work, and constrained workflow augmentation rather than full autonomy.
  3. The most underrated bottleneck is no longer just chips. It is the full chain: advanced packaging, networking, datacenter buildout, and especially electricity and interconnection timing.
  4. My base case for the next 12 months: value capture remains skewed toward compute and distribution owners; many software-layer vendors will discover that “AI feature attach” is easier than AI pricing power.
  5. High-conviction call: reasoning/inference demand is real and still accelerating. Lower-conviction call: agentic enterprise deployment at scale in regulated workflows by year-end 2026.

3) Demand analysis (enterprise adoption, consumer uptake, pricing power, pilot→production)

Facts

  • OpenAI said ChatGPT now serves more than 800 million weekly users and that it has more than 1 million business customers, with more than 7 million ChatGPT for Work seats and ChatGPT Enterprise seats up 9x year over year.
  • OpenAI’s 2025 enterprise report said weekly messages in ChatGPT Enterprise increased roughly 8x over the past year, structured workflow usage rose 19x year-to-date, and average reasoning-token consumption per organization increased roughly 320x in 12 months.
  • That same report said 75% of workers reported AI improved speed or quality, with reported time savings of 40–60 minutes per day.
  • OpenAI’s NBER-linked consumer usage study said ChatGPT had roughly 700 million weekly active users in mid-2025 and that about one third of “Doing” usage was work-related.
  • Anthropic’s January 2026 Economic Index said Claude usage remains concentrated in a limited number of tasks, with the top 10 tasks accounting for 24% of sampled Claude.ai conversations, and that augmentation patterns were just over half of Claude.ai conversations while automation remained dominant in first-party API traffic.

Interpretation

Demand is not speculative anymore. Both consumer scale and enterprise penetration are clearly real. But usage breadth is not the same as monetization quality. The strongest signal is not “people are trying AI”; it is that enterprises are moving from casual prompts into repeatable, structured workflows. That said, current evidence still points to augmentation first, full automation later.

Confidence: High on demand breadth. Confidence: Medium on pricing power durability because vendor disclosures still emphasize adoption metrics more than cohort retention, realized ROI, or renewal quality.

4) Supply analysis (chips, packaging, cloud capacity, power/energy, talent bottlenecks)

Facts

  • NVIDIA reported $68.1 billion of Q4 FY2026 revenue, up 73% year over year, with 75.2% non-GAAP gross margin for the quarter and $215.9 billion of FY2026 revenue, up 65%.
  • Microsoft FY2026 Q2 reported $81.3 billion of revenue, $51.5 billion of Microsoft Cloud revenue, and commercial remaining performance obligation up 110% to $625 billion. On the earnings call, Microsoft said capital expenditures were $37.5 billion and roughly two thirds of capex was on short-lived assets, primarily GPUs and CPUs.
  • Broadcom’s official investor releases indicated AI semiconductor revenue reached roughly $8.2 billion in fiscal Q4 2025 and stayed strong into fiscal Q1 2026, driven by custom accelerators and Ethernet AI switches.
  • TSMC management said it was trying “very hard to narrow the gap, for now and for 2026” and was building many new backend facilities to increase CoWoS capacity because AI demand remained very strong.
  • IEA’s 2025 Energy and AI work said global data-center electricity demand is projected to rise from roughly 460 TWh in 2024 to about 945 TWh by 2030 in its base case, with AI the most important growth driver.

Interpretation

The shortage narrative is evolving, not disappearing. Leading-edge silicon supply is improving, but the binding constraint has broadened into a system bottleneck: packaging, networking, datacenter commissioning, and power. That matters because it raises the all-in cost of useful deployed capacity. In other words, the scarcest product is not chips; it is ready-to-serve AI capacity.

Confidence: High that infra demand remains tight. Confidence: Medium on the pace of easing because the weakest links are increasingly local: permitting, substations, transformers, cooling, and workforce availability.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

  • NVIDIA: still the strongest integrated compute moat because silicon, networking, software stack, ecosystem, and customer urgency all reinforce each other. Main risk: customer concentration and gradual custom-silicon substitution at the margin, not a sudden collapse.
  • Broadcom: highly leveraged to the custom-AI and switching buildout. Moat is narrower than NVIDIA’s but still powerful where hyperscalers want custom architectures and scaled Ethernet fabrics.
  • TSMC: one of the most defensible chokepoints in the stack. If advanced packaging and backend capacity stay constrained, TSMC captures value even if end-market leadership rotates.
  • Microsoft / Google / Amazon: moat is distribution plus cloud control plus enterprise trust. The real battleground is not model quality alone; it is who owns identity, data access, developer workflow, and budget line-items.
  • OpenAI / Anthropic: strong product pull and strong developer mindshare, but long-term moat depends on staying embedded in enterprise systems rather than remaining an expensive intelligence layer behind someone else’s distribution.
  • Enterprise application layer: moats exist where AI is tied to system-of-record data and measurable workflow outcomes. They are weak where AI is just an add-on interface wrapped around commodity models.

7) Financial architecture (capex intensity, unit economics, margin durability, cash-flow quality)

Facts

  • Microsoft’s FY2026 Q2 call showed capex at $37.5 billion in-quarter, with demand continuing to exceed supply.
  • NVIDIA preserved extraordinary profitability even at huge scale, posting 75.2% non-GAAP gross margin in Q4 FY2026.
  • Alphabet’s February 2026 investor commentary pointed to another very large step-up in 2026 AI infrastructure spending, but I am treating the exact figure as lower-confidence here because direct fetch access to the official call materials was blocked by anti-bot controls.

Judgement

The industry is still in a land-grab phase, but investors should stop treating all AI capex as equally good. The key distinction is between capacity built because demand is monetizing well and capacity built because nobody wants to be the one who under-invested. Those are very different return profiles.

My framework is simple:

  • Good capex: rising utilization, improving service mix, visible enterprise conversion, durable gross margin.
  • Bad capex: vague AI revenue language, escalating depreciation/lease burden, and weak free-cash-flow conversion.

The market will likely become less tolerant of pure “build now, explain later” stories over the next 6–12 months.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

U.S. export controls remain a first-order industry variable because they affect both who can buy frontier compute and where global AI clusters can be assembled.

  • The U.S. Federal Register’s January 2025 Framework for Artificial Intelligence Diffusion revised controls on advanced computing ICs and added new controls on model weights for certain advanced closed-weight dual-use AI models.
  • The rule was effective 13 Jan 2025, with a main compliance date of 15 May 2025 and some provisions delayed to 15 Jan 2026.

9) Policy/regulatory landscape and likely pathways

  • The EU AI Act Service Desk says the rollout is progressive: definitions, AI literacy, and prohibitions applied from 2 Feb 2025; general-purpose AI obligations from 2 Aug 2025; the majority of rules and enforcement from 2 Aug 2026; and further high-risk product rules from 2 Aug 2027.
  • This means 2026 is not a distant compliance story. It is the year procurement teams begin to demand implementation evidence, transparency tooling, and operational governance rather than slide-deck promises.

Likely pathway: more guidance, sandboxes, and sector-specific interpretations rather than one clean global regulatory settlement. Operationally, that favors vendors with strong audit trails, policy controls, and enterprise support over raw-model-only vendors.

11) Scenario tree for next 3 / 6 / 12 months with signposts

3 months (to Jun 2026)

Base: infra demand remains strong, but market reward shifts toward execution quality rather than capex headline size. Signposts: hyperscaler commentary on utilization, inference economics, and backlog conversion.

Bull: enterprise software vendors begin disclosing stronger AI monetization cohorts. Bear: power and deployment delays become visible enough to push out usable capacity.

6 months (to Sep 2026)

Base: AI spend stays high, but leadership broadens only selectively into software and services with hard ROI proof. Signposts: renewal commentary, outcome-based pricing, and evidence that pilots convert to standardized internal platforms.

12 months (to Mar 2027)

Base: value capture remains led by compute, packaging, and distribution owners; application-layer winners exist, but are fewer than the market assumes today. Signposts: free-cash-flow quality after heavy buildout, lower-cost inference at scale, and whether agentic workflows become normal inside large enterprises rather than isolated case studies.

12) Actionable monitoring dashboard + “what would change my mind”

Dashboard

  1. Hyperscaler capex vs monetization: Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta capex plans versus disclosed AI revenue or usage-quality metrics.
  2. Infra bottlenecks: NVIDIA lead times, Broadcom AI networking momentum, TSMC packaging commentary, utility/interconnection delays.
  3. Enterprise conversion quality: business seats, paid usage depth, retention, renewal, and movement from copilots to system-level workflows.
  4. Inference economics: cost-per-token, routing efficiency, model-mix improvement, gross margin commentary from model and cloud vendors.
  5. Policy: BIS/Federal Register updates, EU AI Act implementation guidance, and procurement-driven compliance changes.

What would change my mind

  • More bullish on the software layer: broad evidence that enterprise AI revenue is recurring, high-retention, and margin-accretive rather than bundle-subsidized.
  • More cautious on infrastructure: signs that utilization is flattening, customers are optimizing spend faster than expected, or power delays are turning planned demand into deferred demand.
  • More constructive on agents: repeated evidence of low-exception, auditable deployment in regulated workflows beyond coding and internal support.

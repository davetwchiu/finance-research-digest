AI Industry Research Notebook (in-depth, plain-language)

Prepared for David • Tuesday, 3 Mar 2026 (09:30 HKT run) • Method: A/B workflow (Builder + Gate review), source-linked, explicit confidence tags.

1) Executive thesis and key calls

Core call AI is still a multi-year platform buildout, but market leadership is rotating from “who can spend capex fastest” to “who can convert AI demand into durable free cash flow.”

  1. Infrastructure remains the largest near-term profit pool, but valuation upside now depends on utilization quality and customer concentration risk, not pure scarcity.
  2. Enterprise software with workflow control has the best 12-month risk/reward if vendors can prove pilot-to-production conversion and low rework rates.
  3. Hyperscalers are entering a proof-of-economics phase: investors will ask if AI revenue growth is keeping pace with AI capex intensity.
  4. Policy and export controls are now fundamental earnings variables (not side risks), especially for compute supply chains exposed to China restrictions.

Confidence on section call set: Medium (directional), with highest uncertainty on timing of software ROI conversion and policy path.

3) Demand analysis

Facts

  • Microsoft reported $81.3B revenue (+17% YoY) and Azure growth of 39% in FY26 Q2 (quarter ended Dec 2025), indicating enterprise cloud+AI demand remains strong.
  • IBM disclosed a generative AI book of business > $12.5B, suggesting demand is broadening beyond hyperscaler-native buyers.

Interpretation

Current disclosures suggest demand is still healthy, while conversion quality is the key uncertainty. A higher-signal KPI is whether deployments move from innovation budgets to recurring operating budgets with measurable cycle-time or cost reductions.

Confidence

Medium-High on enterprise demand momentum; Medium on broad pilot-to-production conversion rates due to limited standardized disclosure.

4) Supply analysis

Facts

  • NVIDIA FY26 Q4 revenue was $68.1B (+73% YoY); FY26 revenue $215.9B (+65% YoY).
  • Broadcom FY25 Q4 revenue was $18.0B (+28% YoY), and management guided FY26 Q1 AI semiconductor revenue to $8.2B (about double YoY).
  • IEA projects data center electricity demand rising from ~415 TWh (2024) toward ~945 TWh by 2030 in its base case.

Interpretation

Constraints appear to be shifting from pure chip availability toward power, cooling, and integration at usable utilization rates. In several regions, power and permitting are becoming bottlenecks alongside semiconductors.

Confidence

High on chip demand; Medium-High on power bottleneck severity timing by region.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

  • NVDA/AVGO: moat = ecosystem + software + networking integration. Risk = customer insourcing/custom silicon and geopolitical constraints.
  • MSFT/AMZN/GOOG/META/ORCL: moat = distribution + full-stack control. Risk = capex discipline and utilization visibility.
  • AAPL: moat = device install base + private edge inference potential. Risk = slower visible monetization cadence vs cloud peers.
  • IBM/Accenture: moat = enterprise trust + integration depth. Risk = labor-heavy delivery limiting operating leverage.
  • PLTR-style platforms: moat = deployment speed in complex institutions. Risk = concentration and procurement cyclicality.

7) Financial architecture

Facts

  • NVIDIA FY26 non-GAAP gross margin: 71.3% (still elite, though below prior-year peak).
  • Broadcom FY25 Q4 adjusted EBITDA margin: 68%; free cash flow margin ~41%.
  • Microsoft cloud revenue surpassed $50B in quarter, highlighting scale economics in bundled enterprise demand.

Interpretation

The valuation split in 2026 should track three variables: (1) capex intensity versus monetization velocity, (2) gross margin durability under inference cost pressure, and (3) free cash flow conversion after AI infra spend.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

Base case: restrictions on advanced AI compute exports remain tight and selectively tighten further; global supply chains remain investable but with higher compliance friction and regional duplication costs.

  • Scenario A (base): controlled tightening; manageable with product segmentation and regional GTM adaptation.
  • Scenario B (bear): sharper rule expansion (more product classes/end-users restricted) causing inventory and demand dislocations.
  • Scenario C (bull): stable rules + compliance clarity, allowing smoother planning and reduced policy risk premium.

Confidence: Medium (policy path is inherently political and discontinuous).

9) Policy/regulatory landscape

  • EU AI Act prohibitions became effective in Feb 2025; high-risk obligations phase in during 2026–2027.
  • Regulation is increasingly operational: model risk management, traceability, data governance, and human oversight requirements are becoming procurement filters.

Implication: compliance tooling is becoming a revenue enabler, not just a cost center, for enterprise AI vendors.

11) Scenario tree (3 / 6 / 12 months)

3 months

Base: strong infra demand remains intact; software monetization narrative mixed. Signpost: management commentary on inference efficiency and production conversions.

6 months

Base: wider dispersion across AI beneficiaries. Signpost: whether enterprise vendors show measurable ROI metrics (cost-per-case, cycle-time, rework reduction).

12 months

Base: market increasingly rewards cash-flow-quality AI, not narrative AI. Signpost: free cash flow growth consistency versus capex and SBC trend.

12) Actionable monitoring dashboard + “what would change my mind”

Dashboard (weekly/monthly)

  1. Hyperscaler AI demand commentary vs capex guidance.
  2. Supply chain utilization indicators (accelerators, networking, power bring-up timelines).
  3. Enterprise disclosure quality: pilot conversion, renewal economics, and realized productivity metrics.

What would change my mind (bearish → less bearish / bullish → less bullish)

  • More bullish if: large-cap software shows sustained production ROI evidence with improving FCF conversion.
  • More cautious if: AI capex keeps accelerating while gross margins/FCF stagnate and policy friction rises.

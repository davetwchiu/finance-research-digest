1) Executive thesis and key calls

Core thesis AI demand is no longer the debate. The real debate is who keeps the economics once the industry moves from emergency buildout to measured return-on-capital scrutiny. My base case is that the next leg of value capture stays concentrated in compute, packaging, networking, cloud distribution, and workflow owners with real seat expansion. Many software vendors will show usage growth before they show pricing power.

  1. Infrastructure still wins the next 3–6 months. NVIDIA, Broadcom, TSMC advanced packaging, and hyperscaler capacity owners remain the cleanest monetization layer because their demand is visible and paid for now.
  2. Enterprise adoption is real, but the monetization mix is uneven. OpenAI's data show scale and deeper workflow usage, while Anthropic's data still show heavy concentration in a relatively small set of tasks. That is a good setup for selective winners, not a blanket software boom.
  3. The main bottleneck has widened from chips to deployable capacity. Ready-to-serve AI capacity depends on silicon, CoWoS/backend packaging, networking, datacenter commissioning, and power/interconnection timing. The scarce good is no longer just the GPU.
  4. Reasoning demand is real; full enterprise autonomy is still oversold. Higher-value inference is clearly monetizing in coding, research, and scoped workflows. Claims about broad low-supervision agents across regulated enterprise environments still outrun the evidence.
  5. Key market call: over the next 12 months, the market should become less tolerant of capex stories without proof of utilization, margin durability, and cash-flow conversion. That is the hurdle where some AI narratives break.

3) Demand analysis (enterprise adoption, consumer uptake, pricing power, pilot→production conversion)

Facts

  • OpenAI says it now has more than 1 million business customers directly using OpenAI via ChatGPT for Work or the developer platform.
  • OpenAI says ChatGPT serves more than 800 million weekly users, giving it a consumer-to-enterprise distribution flywheel that most rivals do not have.
  • OpenAI says it now has more than 7 million ChatGPT for Work seats, up 40% in two months, and ChatGPT Enterprise seats are up 9x year over year.
  • OpenAI's State of enterprise AI report says weekly messages in ChatGPT Enterprise increased roughly 8x over the past year, structured workflow usage rose 19x year-to-date, and average reasoning-token consumption per organization increased approximately 320x in 12 months.
  • The same OpenAI report says 75% of workers surveyed report improved speed or quality from AI use, with reported time savings of roughly 40–60 minutes per day.
  • Anthropic's January 2026 Economic Index says Claude usage remains concentrated: the top 10 tasks account for 24% of sampled Claude.ai conversations, augmentation is 52% of Claude.ai conversations, and automated use remains dominant in first-party API traffic.

Interpretation

The strongest demand signal is no longer trial activity. It is the move from casual prompting into repeatable structured workflows. OpenAI's data point to deepening production usage, especially in knowledge work and coding-adjacent workflows. Anthropic's data are also useful because they show restraint: usage is broadening, but it is still concentrated in a limited task set. That combination argues for real enterprise adoption, but selective monetization.

Confidence: High that demand breadth is real. Confidence: Medium on long-term pricing power, because vendor disclosures still emphasize usage and seat growth more than renewal economics, churn, and gross-margin quality at the model/application layer.

4) Supply analysis (chips, packaging, cloud capacity, power/energy, talent bottlenecks)

Facts

  • NVIDIA reported $68.1 billion of Q4 FY2026 revenue, up 73% year over year, with 75.2% non-GAAP gross margin. Full-year revenue reached $215.9 billion, up 65%.
  • NVIDIA guided to $78.0 billion ±2% of revenue for Q1 FY2027 and explicitly said it is not assuming Data Center compute revenue from China in that outlook.
  • Microsoft reported fiscal Q2 2026 revenue of $81.3 billion, Microsoft Cloud revenue of $51.5 billion, and commercial remaining performance obligation of $625 billion, up 110%.
  • Microsoft management said quarterly capital expenditure was roughly $37.5 billion, with about two-thirds going to short-lived assets, primarily GPUs and CPUs.
  • Broadcom's fiscal Q1 2026 release was partly blocked by anti-bot controls during this run, so I am carrying forward only the lower-risk conclusion from official investor materials already cited yesterday: AI semiconductor revenue remained very strong into fiscal Q1 2026, led by custom accelerators and AI switching.
  • The IEA's Energy and AI work still frames the macro bottleneck correctly: AI is a meaningful growth driver for data-center electricity demand, and grid/transmission bottlenecks can matter almost as much as chip bottlenecks in determining usable AI capacity.

Interpretation

The supply chain is not normalizing in the way many investors mean when they say “the shortage is easing.” Individual links may improve, but the bottleneck has become multi-stage and local: packaging, networking, datacenter fit-out, utility access, cooling, and interconnection. That means deployable AI capacity remains scarce even when raw silicon supply improves.

This is why infrastructure names still deserve premium attention. The market is not just paying for chips; it is paying for the ability to turn capex into working clusters on time.

Confidence: High that infra demand stays strong near term. Confidence: Medium on how fast bottlenecks ease, because the weakest links are now outside pure semiconductor fabrication.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

  • NVIDIA: still the strongest moat in the stack because silicon, networking, CUDA/software, partner ecosystem, and customer urgency reinforce each other. The real risk is gradual custom silicon dilution at the margin, not a sudden demand collapse.
  • Broadcom: one of the best-positioned beneficiaries of the custom-AI buildout and Ethernet-centric AI networking. Its moat is narrower than NVIDIA's but real where hyperscalers want custom accelerators and large-scale switching fabric.
  • TSMC: remains a chokepoint winner because advanced-node manufacturing plus advanced packaging make it essential regardless of which end customer is strongest.
  • Microsoft / Alphabet / Amazon / Meta: their moat is not just model access. It is distribution, cloud control, data gravity, developer tooling, and the ability to subsidize or bundle AI inside existing spend envelopes.
  • OpenAI / Anthropic: strong product pull and developer relevance, but long-run moat depends on staying embedded in enterprise workflow and distribution channels instead of becoming an expensive upstream intelligence provider.
  • Application vendors: moats are strongest when AI is tied to proprietary data, approvals, compliance, and systems of record. They are weakest when AI is just a new interface over commodity model calls.

7) Financial architecture (capex intensity, unit economics, margin durability, cash-flow quality)

Facts

  • Microsoft's Q2 FY2026 numbers show how capital hungry this cycle is: $37.5 billion of capex in one quarter, with management saying demand continues to exceed supply.
  • NVIDIA's Q4 FY2026 results show that best-in-class infrastructure vendors can still convert AI demand into extraordinary profitability, with 75.2% non-GAAP gross margin.
  • OpenAI and Anthropic usage data both imply rising inference intensity, especially for higher-value reasoning workloads, which matters because inference economics increasingly determine whether software-layer margin stories hold up.

Judgement

The industry's financial test is shifting from who can spend to who can earn through the spend. In practical terms, I would separate AI capex into two buckets:

  • Good capex: utilization is rising, paid usage is deepening, backlog converts, and gross margin remains healthy despite inference growth.
  • Questionable capex: management leans on ambition language, but revenue disclosure is thin, depreciation/lease burden rises, and free cash flow does not improve proportionately.

My bias is that market tolerance for pure “build now, explain later” narratives falls meaningfully over the next 6–12 months.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

Export controls are now a first-order industry variable, not a side constraint.

  • The BIS Framework for Artificial Intelligence Diffusion revised controls on advanced computing ICs and added controls on AI model weights for certain advanced closed-weight dual-use models.
  • The rule took effect on 13 Jan 2025, with a main compliance date of 15 May 2025 and some delayed provisions to 15 Jan 2026.
  • NVIDIA's own Q1 FY2027 outlook explicitly assuming no Data Center compute revenue from China is a concrete reminder that geopolitics is already embedded in company planning, not just in investor hypotheticals.

9) Policy/regulatory landscape and likely pathways

  • The EU AI Act rollout remains progressive: definitions, AI literacy, and prohibitions applied from 2 Feb 2025; general-purpose AI obligations from 2 Aug 2025; the majority of rules and enforcement begin from 2 Aug 2026; and further high-risk product rules apply from 2 Aug 2027.
  • That means 2026 is the year compliance moves from policy discussion to procurement reality. Buyers increasingly want governance evidence, auditability, logging, and controls—not just model quality demos.

Likely pathway: fragmented but tightening implementation across major jurisdictions. The practical winners are vendors that can prove transparency, control, and deployment support. Pure-model vendors with thin enterprise wrappers will have a harder time meeting buyer requirements.

11) Scenario tree for next 3 / 6 / 12 months with signposts

3 months (to Jun 2026)

Base: infrastructure demand stays hot and market leadership remains concentrated in capacity owners and clear monetizers. Signposts: utilization commentary, backlog conversion, and whether inference demand improves economics or merely shifts cost mix.

Bull: application vendors begin disclosing cleaner AI revenue and better renewal/expansion metrics. Bear: datacenter and power bottlenecks push out usable capacity enough to delay monetization.

6 months (to Sep 2026)

Base: value capture broadens selectively into software and enterprise workflow vendors with measurable ROI, but many bundled copilots still fail to show stand-alone pricing power. Signposts: seat growth with retention, workflow standardization, and evidence that pilots convert to operating processes.

12 months (to Mar 2027)

Base: infrastructure, packaging, and distribution still take the largest share of economics, while a narrower-than-expected set of application winners prove durable moats. Signposts: post-buildout free-cash-flow quality, lower-cost inference at scale, and repeatable enterprise agent deployment beyond narrow functions.

12) Actionable monitoring dashboard + “what would change my mind”

Dashboard

  1. Hyperscaler capex vs monetization: Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta capex guidance versus disclosed AI revenue quality, utilization, and margin commentary.
  2. Deployable capacity bottlenecks: NVIDIA lead times, Broadcom AI networking momentum, TSMC packaging commentary, datacenter commissioning, utility/interconnection friction.
  3. Enterprise conversion quality: paid seats, active usage depth, renewal quality, workflow embedding, and whether AI budgets move from experimental to recurring line items.
  4. Inference economics: cost-per-token, routing efficiency, reasoning mix, and whether higher-value use cases offset cost pressure.
  5. Policy / geopolitics: BIS actions, EU AI Act implementation details, and procurement-driven compliance demands.

What would change my mind

  • More bullish on software/apps: broad evidence that AI revenue is recurring, sticky, and margin-accretive rather than mainly bundle-driven.
  • More cautious on infrastructure: signs of flattening utilization, materially faster customer optimization, or power delays turning demand into repeated deferrals.
  • More constructive on agents: repeated proof of low-exception, auditable deployment in regulated workflows outside coding and internal support.

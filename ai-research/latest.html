1) Executive thesis and key calls

Core call AI is still an infrastructure-and-distribution trade first, and only selectively an application-software trade.

Facts

  • NVIDIA reported Q4 FY2026 revenue of $68.1B, Data Center revenue of $62.3B, and non-GAAP gross margin of 75.2%.
  • Microsoft reported FY26 Q2 revenue of $81.3B, Microsoft Cloud revenue of $51.5B, Azure and other cloud services growth of 39%, and commercial remaining performance obligation of $625B, up 110%.
  • OpenAI says it now has more than 1 million business customers, more than 7 million ChatGPT for Work seats, and more than 800 million weekly users.

Interpretation

  1. The market no longer needs proof that AI demand exists. It needs proof of who keeps the economics.
  2. So far, the cleanest proof still sits upstream: compute vendors, cloud owners, and workflow distributors.
  3. The app layer is real, but the broad market is still over-crediting usage growth as if it automatically means durable pricing power.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes hyperscaler and enterprise AI spend stays firm through 2026, without a major policy shock that abruptly changes export-control or compliance burdens.

3) Demand analysis

Facts

  • OpenAI says ChatGPT now serves more than 800 million weekly users.
  • OpenAI says business usage now exceeds 1 million paying business customers.
  • OpenAI says ChatGPT for Work seats reached more than 7 million, up 40% in two months, and Enterprise seats are up 9x YoY.
  • OpenAI’s enterprise report says weekly ChatGPT Enterprise messages increased about 8x over the past year, structured workflows rose 19x year-to-date, and average reasoning-token consumption per organization rose about 320x in 12 months.
  • OpenAI’s worker survey says 75% report improved speed or quality, with typical time savings of about 40–60 minutes per day.
  • Accenture said advanced AI bookings were $2.2B in Q1 FY2026 and revenue reached roughly $1.1B.

Interpretation

Demand has moved beyond experimentation. The important shift is not just more prompts. It is deeper workflow usage, heavier reasoning consumption, and large service contracts to wire AI into actual operations.

My stronger view: enterprise demand is now real enough that the debate has shifted from whether adoption happens to who captures the margin stack. That is a better setup for platform owners than for thin application wrappers.

Scenario assumptions

The base case assumes business spending stays concentrated in coding, research, customer operations, enterprise search, and process automation rather than exploding evenly across all departments.

4) Supply analysis

Facts

  • NVIDIA guided Q1 FY2027 revenue to $78.0B ±2%, which implies no real near-term collapse in AI infrastructure demand.
  • NVIDIA said it is not assuming any Data Center compute revenue from China in that outlook.
  • IEA estimates data-center electricity use at about 415 TWh in 2024 and projects it to roughly 945 TWh by 2030 in its base case, with AI-driven accelerated servers a major driver.
  • The IEA notes data centers can be built in two to three years, while the broader energy system often needs much longer lead times.

Interpretation

The bottleneck is no longer just chips. It is usable capacity: power, interconnect, cooling, siting, and the balance-sheet ability to reserve supply early. That favors NVIDIA, hyperscalers, and large enterprise distributors over smaller model challengers and smaller software vendors.

Strong view: power is becoming the hidden governor on AI growth. Investors who only track model releases are missing the industrial side of this market.

Scenario assumptions

I am not making a specific packaging or foundry-share claim today because I did not fetch clean primary foundry pages in this run. The supply call here is deliberately narrower and better supported.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

Facts

  • NVIDIA still converts demand into very high gross margin and very large free-cash-generation capacity.
  • Microsoft’s cloud revenue scale and RPO growth show unusual ability to fund AI capex and monetize distribution simultaneously.
  • OpenAI has a consumer-to-enterprise flywheel: >800M weekly users feeding >1M business customers.
  • Anthropic’s data show high-value use, but also concentration and limits on present-day autonomy.
  • Accenture is seeing scaled AI implementation demand rather than just advisory interest.

Interpretation

The moat stack looks like this:

  1. Physical moat: compute, energy access, network, and capital.
  2. Distribution moat: cloud contracts, installed enterprise footprint, and procurement trust.
  3. Workflow moat: being embedded in the system where work is approved and recorded.
  4. Model moat: important, but less durable by itself than the market still assumes.

Strong view: the market keeps talking as if the model itself is the business. Most of the time, the business is the distribution rail and workflow control point wrapped around the model.

Scenario assumptions

This view changes if multiple app vendors start showing sustained incremental AI revenue with stable or improving margins, not just higher usage stats.

7) Financial architecture

Facts

  • NVIDIA’s Q4 FY2026 non-GAAP gross margin was 75.2%.
  • Microsoft Cloud revenue was $51.5B in FY26 Q2, and commercial RPO rose to $625B.
  • Accenture said advanced AI revenue reached approximately $1.1B in the quarter and cumulative AI bookings reached approximately $11.5B across 11,000 projects.

Interpretation

The financial architecture of AI still looks top-heavy. Upstream vendors with scarce assets and large balance sheets can invest first and collect first. Services firms can monetize the transition. Much of the app layer is still proving whether usage intensity can survive after inference, support, and discounting costs are counted honestly.

My bias remains skeptical of software narratives that celebrate adoption but do not discuss gross margin after model costs, retention after bundle pricing, or the incremental cost of enterprise support and controls.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes inference costs improve, but not fast enough to erase the strategic advantage of scale buyers.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

Facts

  • BIS revised controls on advanced computing ICs and added new controls on AI model weights for certain advanced closed-weight dual-use models.
  • The rule became effective on 13 Jan 2025, with a main compliance date of 15 May 2025 and some delayed provisions to 15 Jan 2026.
  • NVIDIA’s current outlook assumes no China Data Center compute revenue.

Interpretation

Export controls are now an operating variable, not a side note. The strategic consequence is simple: global AI will not be one market. It will be segmented by policy regime, chip access, and approved deployment paths.

Scenario assumptions

Base case assumes the U.S. keeps a hard strategic posture on frontier compute and model diffusion, with periodic tightening but not a total global freeze.

9) Policy / regulatory landscape and likely pathways

Facts

  • European Commission says the AI Act prohibitions became effective in February 2025.
  • General-purpose AI rules became effective in August 2025.
  • High-risk AI rules take effect in August 2026 and August 2027.
  • The listed obligations for high-risk AI include risk management, dataset quality, logging, documentation, deployer information, human oversight, robustness, cybersecurity, and accuracy.

Interpretation

Regulation is quietly strengthening the case for incumbent enterprise platforms. Compliance is expensive, operational, and continuous. Vendors that can bundle model capability with auditability, logging, access controls, and policy tooling should win procurement cycles.

Scenario assumptions

I assume EU enforcement is uneven at first, but strong enough to shape product roadmaps and enterprise buying criteria well before the full deadline stack arrives.

11) Scenario tree for next 3 / 6 / 12 months

3 months

  • Base (55%): infrastructure leaders and cloud distributors keep posting the cleanest monetization. Enterprise AI usage deepens, but app-layer revenue proof remains selective.
  • Bull (25%): several workflow vendors start posting measurable incremental AI revenue with decent unit economics, pushing the market toward a broader software rerating.
  • Bear (20%): energy/capacity constraints and policy frictions slow deployments enough to expose fragile downstream AI narratives.

6 months

  • Base (50%): AI spending stays strong, but value capture remains concentrated in NVIDIA, hyperscalers, and major workflow/distribution rails.
  • Bull (20%): enterprise production deployments broaden materially beyond coding, search, and support, validating a bigger software capture layer.
  • Bear (30%): capex digestion and policy friction compress sentiment before downstream monetization catches up.

12 months

  • Base (45%): AI is economically large, but still more concentrated than the broad equity narrative assumes.
  • Bull (25%): leading software/workflow vendors prove AI can be margin-accretive rather than just engagement-accretive.
  • Bear (30%): the industry discovers that abundant model access without abundant cheap power and strong workflow ownership is a worse business than expected.

What would change my mind

  • More bullish on the app layer: if several software vendors show recurring AI revenue that is incremental, sticky, and margin-resilient after model costs.
  • Less bullish on infrastructure: if infrastructure margins crack while app-layer capture broadens faster than expected.
  • More bullish on broad AI diffusion: if public data show genuine spread into ordinary enterprise operating workflows rather than mostly coding, search, and specialist knowledge work.

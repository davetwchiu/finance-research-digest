AI Industry Research Notebook (In-Depth)

Prepared for David • Tuesday, 24 Feb 2026 (08:15 HKT reference) • Plain-language deep dive with explicit analyst calls and falsifiable signposts.

How to read this notebook

This is intentionally long-form. It is written as a decision notebook, not a news summary. The goal is to separate what is monetizing now from what is still narrative, and to map where value capture is likely to concentrate over the next 3/6/12 months.

Core call
AI remains a real platform shift, but returns are getting more selective and increasingly tied to distribution + workflow ownership rather than raw model scores.
Base case
2026 is the year of conversion pressure: markets will demand proof that pilot spend becomes recurring production revenue.
Main risk
If demand growth slows before supply constraints clear, we can get simultaneous margin compression in infra and de-rating in application multiples.

(1) Executive thesis and key calls

Executive thesis

The AI stack is moving from capacity race to economic race. In 2023–2025, the market rewarded who could secure GPUs, launch frontier models, and announce AI roadmaps. In 2026, the bar rises: who can deliver durable margins after accounting for inference cost, sales friction, governance overhead, and customer churn?

My central view: the biggest winners over the next 12 months are not necessarily the companies with the best models; they are the ones with the best distribution, integration depth, and cost discipline.

Key calls (strong views)

  • Call #1 — Value capture shifts up-stack: Compute suppliers still print strong revenue, but incremental alpha rotates toward software firms that own user workflows (Microsoft 365/Copilot stack, ServiceNow, Salesforce, Adobe, Atlassian, enterprise vertical AI specialists).
  • Call #2 — Frontier model quality is converging faster than pricing: A few labs remain ahead (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind), but many workloads are now “good enough,” weakening premium pricing unless tied to proprietary data, trust, or distribution.
  • Call #3 — Inference economics becomes the critical battleground: The era of “train once, monetize forever” is over-simplified. Real profitability depends on routing, caching, quantization, model mix, and product design that limits wasteful token usage.
  • Call #4 — Enterprise conversion is improving but uneven: Best-in-class conversion from pilot to production is happening in customer support, software engineering copilots, search/knowledge work, and document automation. Lagging categories: broad “AI assistants” without workflow embedding.
  • Call #5 — Geopolitics is now a first-order valuation variable: Export controls, cloud sovereignty, and energy-security constraints can change supply assumptions and revenue addressability quickly.

Implication by ecosystem names

NVIDIA/TSMC/ASML/AMD/Broadcom: still central to the picks-and-shovels layer, but expectations are high; execution misses can re-rate quickly.

Microsoft/Amazon/Google: best positioned to monetize AI through bundled distribution and enterprise channels, but must prove AI margins are additive, not cannibalistic.

OpenAI/Anthropic/xAI/Mistral/Cohere: productization and distribution partnerships matter more than benchmark headlines.

ServiceNow/Salesforce/Adobe/Intuit/Palantir/SAP/Oracle: strong chance to compound if AI is embedded into existing mission-critical workflows with measurable ROI.

(3) Demand analysis

Enterprise adoption: from experimentation to procurement discipline

Most large enterprises are now in their second wave of AI programs. Wave 1 (2023–2024) was discovery and pilots. Wave 2 (2025–2026) is selective rollout with procurement controls and ROI requirements. CIOs are asking more direct questions: “What workflow gets faster? How many FTE-equivalents? What error rates? What compliance burden?”

Where adoption is genuinely working

  • Customer support: AI triage + draft response + call summarization is producing measurable handle-time reduction.
  • Software engineering copilots: strongest where codebase quality and internal standards are high.
  • Document-heavy operations: legal review, claims processing, contract extraction, KYC onboarding.
  • Internal knowledge search: enterprise RAG stacks with permissions-aware retrieval show practical productivity gains.

Where demand still looks soft / fragile

  • Generic chatbots not embedded into daily workflow systems.
  • Consumer AI subscriptions with weak retention beyond early adopters.
  • Agentic automation claims with limited production reliability and high exception-handling cost.

Pricing power

Pricing power is splitting:

  • Strong: products tied to mission-critical workflows and compliance-heavy contexts (higher switching cost).
  • Weakening: standalone assistant SKUs where alternatives are close enough and switching is easy.

Pilot-to-production conversion: key reality check

The crucial KPI for 2026 is conversion quality, not pilot count. Healthy conversion profile:

  • Pilot in < 90 days
  • Clear KPI baseline before deployment
  • Cross-functional owner (IT + business unit + risk/compliance)
  • Budget line moved from innovation fund to operating budget

Weak profile: indefinite sandbox usage, no owner, no budget transfer, no integration roadmap.

(4) Supply analysis

Chips and packaging

Supply constraints are easing at the margin but remain binding at the high end. Advanced packaging (CoWoS-like capacity), HBM memory, and high-performance networking continue to constrain full-system availability. The bottleneck has moved from “can I buy a GPU?” to “can I deploy complete AI clusters at target utilization?”

Cloud capacity

Hyperscalers expanded aggressively, but capacity utilization quality now matters more than headline capacity growth. If utilization falls due to overbuild or weaker enterprise demand conversion, cloud AI margins can compress quickly.

Power and energy

Power is becoming a strategic asset. Data center site selection is now deeply tied to grid reliability, permitting speed, cooling design, and long-term power purchase agreements. AI capex cannot scale infinitely without transmission upgrades and local permitting alignment.

Talent bottlenecks

The bottleneck is no longer only model researchers. Scarcity is now acute in AI product engineers, inference optimization specialists, security/compliance practitioners, and domain experts who can redesign workflows. This favors companies with deep internal platforms and strong developer ecosystems.

Analyst view

Supply-side easing alone will not destroy pricing. It will redistribute economics from scarce hardware toward software and services that can convert cheaper inference into customer value. Lower compute cost is bullish for usage growth, but bearish for undifferentiated model pricing.

(5) Technology frontier and practical breakthroughs

What is hype

  • Benchmark theater: small benchmark gains marketed as broad commercial breakthroughs.
  • “Fully autonomous agents” claims: still brittle for complex workflows requiring deterministic control and auditability.
  • One-model-does-everything narratives: enterprise stacks increasingly use model routing, not single-model dependence.

What is monetizing now

  • Inference optimization: quantization, speculative decoding, caching, distillation, and workload-specific serving stacks.
  • Data layer improvements: better retrieval quality, permissions-aware indexing, and observability around hallucination/error modes.
  • Workflow-native AI UX: embedding AI into existing software surfaces rather than asking users to switch contexts.
  • Multimodal enterprise use cases: document, voice, image workflows with strict governance.

(6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

Compute layer

Moat: ecosystem lock-in (CUDA/toolchains), manufacturing partnerships, software stack maturity, and systems integration capability.

Challenge: customer concentration and custom silicon by hyperscalers may cap long-run pricing power.

Model layer

Moat: safety/reliability track record, enterprise trust, proprietary data partnerships, and distribution channels.

Challenge: rapid capability diffusion and open-weight alternatives reduce defensibility of “model quality alone.”

Application layer

Moat: owning workflow, identity/access integration, and deep domain data.

Challenge: fast feature parity; many app vendors risk becoming thin wrappers unless they control process and data gravity.

Services layer

Moat: client relationships, industry specialization, and delivery capability across legacy systems.

Challenge: labor-intensive economics and potential margin dilution as clients demand outcome-based pricing.

Hard view

Durable moat in AI is increasingly a system moat, not a single-asset moat. The company that controls distribution + data + workflow + compliance gets paid repeatedly; others fight for episodic revenue.

(7) Financial architecture

Capex intensity

AI infrastructure remains capex-heavy. The key question is no longer whether capex is large; it is whether revenue quality and gross margin trajectory can justify that capex over a multi-year cycle. Investors should track capex-to-incremental-gross-profit, not just capex growth rates.

Unit economics framework (practical)

  • Revenue side: ARPU uplift from AI features, seat expansion, usage-based monetization, churn reduction.
  • Cost side: inference cost per task, support overhead, compliance/monitoring cost, cloud pass-through exposure.
  • Margin durability: can vendor lower cost per output faster than price declines?

Cash-flow quality

High-quality AI cash flow has three signatures:

  1. Recurring production usage (not one-off pilots)
  2. Visible renewals with expanding scope
  3. Improving gross margin despite scaling usage

Where financial risk hides

  • Capitalized AI development masking true run-rate costs
  • Bundled pricing that delays clear monetization signals
  • Deferred revenue growth from discounts that may not renew at list price

Strong analyst stance

In 2026, “AI story with weak free-cash-flow conversion” should be discounted more aggressively than in prior years. Liquidity can no longer carry every narrative. Evidence beats storytelling.

(9) Policy/regulatory landscape and likely pathways

Regulatory direction of travel

Regulation is moving from principle to implementation. Expect increased requirements around data provenance, model risk management, auditability, sector-specific compliance, and consumer protection disclosures.

Likely pathway (next 12 months)

  • More sector-specific guidance (finance, healthcare, public sector)
  • Procurement standards that require demonstrable governance controls
  • Greater legal clarity on synthetic media labeling and IP liability boundaries

Commercial implication

Compliance is shifting from “cost center” to “sales enabler.” Vendors with strong governance tooling can close enterprise deals faster and at better economics.

(11) Scenario tree for next 3/6/12 months with signposts

3 months (near-term): “proof season”

Base: revenue beats concentrated in infra and select enterprise software names; mixed consumer AI monetization. Bull: clear re-acceleration in enterprise production rollouts. Bear: weak conversion metrics and surprise margin pressure.

Signposts: management commentary on pilot-to-production ratios, AI attach rates, and cost-per-inference trends.

6 months (mid-term): “economics season”

Base: valuation dispersion widens; market rewards measurable ROI and penalizes vague AI narratives. Bull: falling inference costs plus stable pricing expands margins. Bear: price competition outruns cost declines.

Signposts: gross margin trajectories, contract renewals, cloud utilization commentary, and customer expansion cohorts.

12 months (strategic horizon): “moat reset”

Base: stable leaders emerge across each layer; some former high-flyers become consolidation targets. Bull: coordinated infra + software + services adoption drives a durable productivity cycle. Bear: macro slowdown plus geopolitical shock hits capex confidence.

Signposts: free-cash-flow conversion durability, regulatory clarity, and cross-border supply chain resilience.

What would change my mind (explicit falsifiers)

  • Become more bullish quickly if enterprise renewal/expansion data shows broad, not narrow, ROI realization and if inference margins improve despite price pressure.
  • Become more defensive if capex guidance remains elevated while AI revenue contribution and cash conversion disappoint for two consecutive reporting cycles.
  • Upgrade model-layer outlook if leading labs demonstrate sustained pricing power without bundling and with improving gross margins.
  • Downgrade infra cyclicality risk if supply-chain bottlenecks ease in a way that supports both volume growth and stable pricing (rather than pure commoditization).

Bottom-line positioning view

Best risk/reward: companies that combine distribution, workflow embedding, and governance trust while proving improving unit economics.

Most fragile setups: valuation-rich stories with weak production conversion and unclear cash-flow path.

One-sentence conclusion: AI is not “over”; it is maturing—and maturation always shifts power from storytellers to operators.

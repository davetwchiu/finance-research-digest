1) Executive thesis and key calls

Base case The AI industry is moving out of the "more GPUs = more upside" phase and into a tougher phase: which layers keep economic rents after capital intensity, inference cost, and regulatory friction are all priced in. The answer is still infrastructure-heavy today, but the reason has shifted. It is no longer just chip scarcity. It is the scarcity of deployable, power-ready, networked, enterprise-usable AI capacity.

  1. Near-term winners are still infrastructure chokepoints. NVIDIA, Broadcom, hyperscaler capacity owners, and advanced-manufacturing/packaging beneficiaries remain the cleanest monetizers because revenue is visible now and demand is tied to real budgets, not pilot decks.
  2. Enterprise demand is broadening, but software monetization is still selective. OpenAI and Anthropic both show deeper enterprise usage, but Anthropic's usage concentration data is a warning that a lot of value still sits in a limited set of high-productivity tasks rather than in universal workflow replacement.
  3. The bottleneck has widened from chips to full-stack deployment. Silicon matters, but so do networking, packaging, rack power, datacenter commissioning, cooling, and governance/compliance. That keeps infrastructure pricing power firmer than many software bulls assume.
  4. Reasoning and agents are commercially real, but only in bounded environments. Coding, research, support, and structured internal workflows are monetizing. Grand claims about broad low-supervision enterprise autonomy still outrun the evidence.
  5. Main market call: over the next 6–12 months, investors will increasingly separate productive AI capex from aspirational AI capex. Companies that cannot connect spend to utilization, margin durability, and cash generation will de-rate first.

3) Demand analysis (enterprise adoption, consumer uptake, pricing power, pilot→production conversion)

Facts

  • OpenAI says more than 1 million business customers are directly paying OpenAI through ChatGPT for Work or the developer platform.
  • OpenAI says ChatGPT serves more than 800 million weekly users, which matters because user familiarity lowers enterprise rollout friction.
  • OpenAI says it now has more than 7 million ChatGPT for Work seats, up 40% in two months, and ChatGPT Enterprise seats are up 9x year over year.
  • OpenAI's 2025 enterprise report says weekly messages in ChatGPT Enterprise rose about 8x over the past year, structured workflow usage rose 19x year-to-date, and average reasoning-token consumption per organization rose about 320x in 12 months.
  • In OpenAI's enterprise survey, 75% of workers said AI improved speed or quality, with reported time savings around 40–60 minutes per day.
  • Anthropic's January 2026 Economic Index says Claude usage remains concentrated: the top 10 tasks account for 24% of sampled Claude.ai conversations and 32% of first-party API traffic; augmentation made up 52% of Claude.ai conversations while automation remained dominant in API traffic.

Interpretation

The demand debate is basically over. Adoption is broad and deepening. The harder question is where usage converts into durable economics. OpenAI's data are bullish on deployment depth. Anthropic's data are the useful reality check: even with powerful models, enterprise value remains concentrated in a relatively narrow set of high-return tasks, especially coding and technical work.

High confidence that enterprise demand is real and expanding. Medium confidence that broad software-layer pricing power will match current enthusiasm, because vendor disclosures still emphasize usage growth more than renewal quality, churn, and incremental margin.

4) Supply analysis (chips, packaging, cloud capacity, power/energy, talent bottlenecks)

Facts

  • NVIDIA reported $68.1 billion of Q4 FY2026 revenue, up 73% year over year, with 75.2% non-GAAP gross margin. Full-year revenue reached $215.9 billion, up 65%.
  • NVIDIA guided to $78.0 billion ±2% for Q1 FY2027 and explicitly said it is not assuming any Data Center compute revenue from China in that outlook.
  • Broadcom reported fiscal Q1 2026 revenue of $19.3 billion, up 29% year over year. It said AI semiconductor revenue was $8.4 billion, up 106%, and guided to $10.7 billion of AI semiconductor revenue in Q2.
  • Microsoft reported fiscal Q2 2026 revenue of $81.3 billion, Microsoft Cloud revenue of $51.5 billion, and commercial remaining performance obligation of $625 billion, up 110%.
  • Microsoft management commentary attached to the quarter pointed to roughly $37.5 billion of capex and finance leases in the quarter, underscoring how fast hyperscaler AI build-out is scaling.
  • The IEA's Energy and AI work remains directionally important even though direct fetch was blocked in this run: AI-driven data center electricity demand is large enough that power, transmission, and grid timelines are now core AI capacity constraints rather than side issues.

Interpretation

The supply story is no longer just about chip availability. The scarce asset is usable AI capacity delivered on time. That includes silicon, networking, packaging, datacenter fit-out, and power. This matters because it preserves infra pricing power longer than a simple “GPU shortage fades, margins collapse” story would suggest.

High confidence that infrastructure demand remains strong through the next several quarters. Medium confidence on the exact rate at which packaging and power bottlenecks ease because direct primary-source access to some supply-chain pages was blocked during this run.

6) Competitive dynamics and moats by segment

  • NVIDIA: strongest moat in the stack today because hardware, software, networking, ecosystem, and customer urgency reinforce one another. The threat is not a sudden collapse; it is gradual dilution from custom silicon where the largest buyers can justify it.
  • Broadcom: one of the cleanest beneficiaries of the custom-accelerator and AI-networking buildout. Its moat is narrower than NVIDIA's, but where hyperscalers want merchant switching plus custom silicon enablement, it is very real.
  • Hyperscalers: Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta have a distribution moat that many model companies do not. They can bundle AI into existing cloud and software spend, control capacity, and absorb long payback periods better than startups.
  • OpenAI / Anthropic: both have strong product pull, but long-run economics depend on whether they remain embedded in enterprise workflow and distribution rather than becoming expensive upstream model suppliers.
  • Application vendors: moats are strongest where AI is tied to proprietary data, approvals, compliance logs, and system-of-record placement. They are weak when AI is just a prettier front end over commodity model calls.
  • Open ecosystems: increasingly important as a pricing and margin cap on closed-model vendors. Even when they do not dominate revenue, they reduce the industry's ability to sustain model-layer supernormal margins.

7) Financial architecture (capex intensity, unit economics, margin durability, cash-flow quality)

Facts

  • NVIDIA is proving that the best-positioned infra vendor can still turn AI demand into extraordinary profitability, with 75.2% Q4 non-GAAP gross margin.
  • Broadcom is proving a second point: AI infra demand does not only accrue to the GPU incumbent. Its Q1 free cash flow of $8.0 billion and 68% adjusted EBITDA margin show real cash conversion from AI networking and custom silicon exposure.
  • Microsoft's quarterly capex scale shows how expensive this cycle has become. That is bullish for suppliers, but it also raises the bar for proving return on invested capital at the hyperscaler and software layers.
  • OpenAI and Anthropic usage trends imply rising inference intensity. That is strategically bullish for demand, but it also means software and model-layer margin durability will depend more on routing, pricing, utilization, and compute efficiency than on user growth alone.

Judgement

The key split is between cash-flowing AI infrastructure and story-driven AI spending. Infrastructure names with visible utilization can still justify premium multiples. Software and platform names need to clear a harder test now: can they convert AI engagement into recurring revenue that is not eaten by inference cost and support burden?

My bias remains that the market is underestimating how quickly investors will punish capex without operating leverage.

8) Geopolitics and export-control scenarios

Export controls are not background noise anymore. They are shaping product design, regional deployment, revenue assumptions, and valuation discounts.

  • The BIS Framework for Artificial Intelligence Diffusion revised controls on advanced computing ICs and added controls on AI model weights for certain advanced closed-weight dual-use AI models.
  • The rule became effective on 13 Jan 2025, with a main compliance date of 15 May 2025 and some delayed provisions to 15 Jan 2026.
  • NVIDIA's own outlook excluding China data center compute revenue is the cleanest real-world proof that export controls are now embedded in company planning.

9) Policy/regulatory landscape and likely pathways

  • The EU AI Act applies progressively: definitions, AI literacy, and prohibitions from 2 Feb 2025; general-purpose AI obligations from 2 Aug 2025; the majority of rules and enforcement from 2 Aug 2026; and further high-risk product rules from 2 Aug 2027.
  • That means 2026 is the year when procurement, governance, documentation, and transparency matter more. Selling “best model” without showing controls is becoming a weaker enterprise sales pitch.
  • US policy is less settled at the broad AI-governance layer, but export-control policy is already concrete enough to shape company behavior.

Likely pathway: uneven but tightening regulation. The practical winners are vendors that can document data handling, model lineage, access controls, logging, and safe deployment. Compliance is turning from drag into moat.

11) Scenario tree for next 3 / 6 / 12 months with signposts

3 months (to Jun 2026)

Base: infrastructure leadership holds, especially where vendors can show capacity conversion and revenue visibility. Signposts: NVIDIA/Broadcom follow-through, hyperscaler capex commentary, and any sign that power or commissioning delays are pushing revenue realization to the right.

Bull: software vendors begin showing cleaner AI monetization metrics rather than vague engagement anecdotes. Bear: investors start treating hyperscaler AI spend as capacity overshoot rather than strategic lead.

6 months (to Sep 2026)

Base: enterprise winners narrow. More buyers move from experimentation to standardized workflows, but pricing power proves strongest where AI is embedded in systems of record or distribution bundles. Signposts: renewals, seat expansion, disclosed ROI, and inference-cost discipline.

12 months (to Mar 2027)

Base: infrastructure, distribution, and a narrower set of workflow owners keep most of the economics. The software field is larger in users than in profits. Signposts: cash-flow quality after the spending wave, evidence of durable agent deployment, and whether model routing commoditizes more of the model layer than expected.

12) Actionable monitoring dashboard + “what would change my mind”

Dashboard

  1. Hyperscaler capex vs monetization: Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta spending versus disclosed AI revenue quality and utilization commentary.
  2. AI infra revenue conversion: NVIDIA data center growth, Broadcom AI semiconductor trajectory, networking attach, and any evidence of digestion.
  3. Enterprise conversion quality: paid seats, active usage depth, structured workflow adoption, renewals, and whether AI budgets become recurring line items.
  4. Inference economics: routing efficiency, reasoning mix, pricing changes, and whether higher-value tasks offset compute cost.
  5. Policy / geopolitics: BIS actions, EU AI Act implementation details, and procurement-led compliance demands.

What would change my mind

  • More bullish on software/apps: broad evidence that AI revenue is recurring, sticky, and margin-accretive rather than mostly bundled or promotional.
  • More cautious on infrastructure: signs of faster-than-expected customer optimization, slower utilization, or repeated capacity deployment delays.
  • More constructive on broad enterprise agents: repeated proof of low-exception, auditable deployment in regulated workflows outside coding, search, and internal support.

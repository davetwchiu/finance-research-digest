Defence Industry Research Notebook

Date: 2026-02-24 (HKT) • Plain-language, opinionated sector view for operators and investors.

Executive Take (Strong View)

My core call: this is still an execution market, not a storytelling market. Defence demand is real and multi-year, but equity winners will be the companies that can actually convert funded demand into delivered units and cash, on schedule, through constrained supply chains.

I am structurally bullish on the sector’s top-line durability, but selective on quality. I prefer names with (1) hard program access, (2) factory throughput proof, and (3) repeatable cash conversion. I avoid “AI/autonomy” names that still depend on pilots and demos with weak procurement pathways.

Bias: Positive demand / selective on valuation

1) Global Demand Trend: What Is Actually Growing?

Demand is being pulled by four durable forces:

  • Replenishment: munitions and missile inventories were drawn down faster than peacetime planning models assumed.
  • Deterrence reset: Europe and Indo-Pacific both moved from “efficiency posture” to “resilience posture.”
  • Kill-chain speed: armed forces are buying speed of decision and connectivity, not just platforms.
  • Interoperability pressure: allies are standardizing data links, secure comms, and ISR sharing.

Analyst opinion: the demand cycle is less cyclical than before because inventories, readiness, and software-defined warfare create recurring spending needs. This is not a one-year spike. It is a five-year rebuilding plus modernization arc.

2) Procurement Cycle Dynamics: Why Revenue Timing Still Surprises

Investors often underestimate how policy process drives timing. Procurement remains staircase-shaped: budget request → authorization → appropriation → contract award → production ramp → acceptance/payment. Earnings volatility mostly happens in the middle of this chain.

3) Technology & Program Shift: Where Budget Is Rotating

3.1 Autonomy

Spending is shifting from exquisite, few-in-number assets toward mixed fleets that include attritable autonomous systems. The value is not in one drone; it is in how autonomy scales ISR, decoys, and strike support in contested environments.

3.2 Electronic Warfare (EW)

EW is moving from niche payload to baseline survivability layer. Jamming resistance, spectrum awareness, and adaptive waveforms are now procurement priorities.

3.3 ISR

ISR spend favors persistent sensing plus data fusion. Satellites, airborne sensors, and edge compute are being integrated into faster targeting loops.

3.4 Secure Communications

Secure comms has become mission-critical infrastructure: anti-jam SATCOM, encrypted tactical networks, and zero-trust architectures for coalition operations.

Analyst opinion: technology winners are not necessarily the best algorithms; they are the teams with integration credibility, certification history, and field support capacity. In defence, “works in theatre” beats “works in demo.”

5) Capex Discipline & Cash-Conversion Quality

In this cycle, capex is not optional. The question is whether capex buys durable throughput or just temporarily relieves bottlenecks.

  • Good capex: debottlenecking known constraints, automation in repeat processes, supplier tooling support, test capacity expansion with clear utilization path.
  • Bad capex: prestige projects, low-utilization expansions, or spending ahead of firm funded orders.

Cash-conversion checklist: funded backlog ratio, milestone quality, working-capital drag during ramps, fixed-price contract mix, and claim recovery discipline.

Analyst opinion: I reward free-cash-flow reliability more than nominal EBITDA growth in this sector. If backlog grows but cash slips repeatedly, that is execution risk wearing a growth mask.

6) Policy, Regulation & Geopolitics

  • Export controls: can block upside or redirect demand to domestic substitutes.
  • Allied burden sharing: improves aggregate demand but changes supplier mix and local-content requirements.
  • Industrial policy: reshoring and security-of-supply goals support capex, but can raise near-term costs.
  • Election/fiscal cycles: can delay appropriations even when strategic consensus is strong.
  • Regional flashpoints: increase urgency but also increase sanction/compliance complexity.

Analyst opinion: geopolitics is now both demand catalyst and operating risk. Treat it as two-sided, not a one-way bullish trigger.

Bottom Line

Defence remains one of the few sectors with genuine demand visibility tied to state-level strategic priorities. But the easy money from thematic rerating is mostly behind us. The next leg belongs to execution leaders.

Portfolio stance: overweight high-confidence program operators, market-weight broad primes, underweight pre-scale narrative names until procurement proof and cash conversion improve.

Defence Industry Research Notebook

Date: 2026-02-25 (HKT) • Plain-language, full-length, opinionated sector notebook.

Executive View (Strong Opinion)

My core call: defence is still in a multi-year demand upcycle, but this phase is no longer “buy the theme and wait.” It is now an execution-and-cash-conversion market.

I remain constructive on sector demand through 2026–2028, but I am selective. Winners should be firms with funded backlog quality, industrial throughput proof, and repeatable free-cash-flow conversion. Losers will be firms with strong narratives but weak procurement conversion and fragile supply chains.

Bias: positive on demand, strict on execution quality and valuation discipline

1) Global Demand Trend: Still Strong, but More Segmented

Demand is being sustained by a combination of structural and cyclical drivers:

  • Inventory rebuild: missile and munition stockpiles remain below desired readiness levels in multiple allied countries.
  • Deterrence reset: Europe and Indo-Pacific continue to move from “efficiency doctrine” to “resilience doctrine.”
  • Alliance interoperability: coalition operations are forcing spend on compatible comms, shared ISR standards, and data links.
  • Shorter tech refresh cycles: software-defined systems are making upgrade cadence faster than traditional platform replacement cycles.

Analyst view: the demand cycle is durable, but no longer broad-based in equity terms. Investors are already paying for “defence growth.” Future outperformance comes from operational delivery, not from macro headlines alone.

2) Procurement Cycle Dynamics: Why Revenue Still Arrives in Steps

Procurement remains staircase-shaped: requirement → authorization → appropriation → award → test/qualification → production acceptance → cash collection. Revenue timing surprises mostly happen in the middle stages.

3) Technology and Program Shift: Where Budget is Actually Rotating

3.1 Autonomy

Budget is shifting from “single exquisite platform logic” toward distributed systems and attritable autonomous assets. The edge is no longer hardware novelty; it is command-and-control integration, reliability in contested environments, and lifecycle support.

3.2 Electronic Warfare (EW)

EW is becoming baseline survivability, not a niche add-on. Adaptive jamming resistance, spectrum intelligence, and reprogrammable effects are becoming procurement priorities across air, land, maritime, and unmanned systems.

3.3 ISR (Intelligence, Surveillance, Reconnaissance)

ISR is moving toward persistent sensing plus fusion. Spend is increasingly tied to the kill-chain speed question: can the force sense, decide, and cue effects faster than the adversary?

3.4 Secure Communications

Secure comms is now strategic infrastructure. Anti-jam SATCOM, hardened tactical networks, resilient data links, and zero-trust architecture are core procurement items, not optional upgrades.

Analyst view: the market still overpays for “AI words” and underweights integration depth. In defence, demo performance is cheap; validated field integration is expensive and moat-like.

5) Capex and Cash-Conversion Quality: The Real Separation Factor

Capex is essential in this cycle. The issue is not “is capex rising?” but “is capex buying durable throughput and better cash conversion?”

  • High-quality capex: debottlenecking known constraints, test infrastructure expansion with utilization visibility, production automation tied to defect reduction.
  • Low-quality capex: speculative expansion ahead of funded demand, prestige projects, and capex without measurable cycle-time improvement.

Cash-conversion stress test

  • Funded backlog to total backlog ratio
  • FCF / net income consistency (rolling 4-quarter view)
  • Working-capital drag during ramp phases
  • Fixed-price exposure and claim-recovery history
  • Milestone structure quality and acceptance timing reliability

Analyst view: I prefer a slower top-line grower with clean cash conversion over a faster grower with recurring “next quarter” explanations.

6) Policy, Regulation, and Geopolitics: Two-Sided Force

  • Export controls and licensing: can cap upside in some geographies while accelerating local substitute demand elsewhere.
  • Alliance burden-sharing: supports aggregate demand but increases local-content obligations and supplier localization pressure.
  • Industrial policy: reshoring and “security of supply” support capex, but can raise near-term cost base and execution complexity.
  • Fiscal politics: strategic consensus may stay intact while appropriation timing creates quarterly noise.
  • Regional flashpoints: urgency drives procurement speed, but sanctions/compliance risk rises in parallel.

Analyst view: geopolitics should be modeled as both a demand catalyst and an operating risk amplifier. One-sided “geopolitical premium” frameworks are too simplistic.

10) Portfolio Implications (Opinionated)

  • Prefer: companies with high-quality funded backlog, visible production ramp, and repeatable FCF conversion.
  • Neutral: broad primes where valuation already prices stability but upside is tied to execution beats.
  • Avoid/underweight: pre-scale autonomy/AI names without durable program-of-record pathways and weak cash discipline.

Bottom line: the sector is still investable, but alpha from here comes from industrial execution and cash realization, not thematic momentum alone.
